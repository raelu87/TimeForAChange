WHAT IS SCHOOL BULLYING?

School bullying refers to a single or multiple deliberate or malicious act of bullying or insulting by one party of students (an individual or a group) inside and outside the campus through physical, language and Internet means, causing physical and psychological injury, property loss or mental damage to the other party (individual or group).

HOW DOES BULLYING AFFECT PEOPLE?

Bullying affects young people in many different ways. It can impact on their self-esteem, emotional wellbeing, education and life outside of school. Young people experiencing bullying may feel that they aren’t worth help or that nobody likes them. They may feel self-conscious or embarrassed lots of the time. They may also feel scared, sad or overwhelmed, and find it difficult to sleep or eat. Many young people who have been bullied find it really hard to ever feel safe or confident in anything they do, leading them to isolate ourselves from others and to give up the things they enjoy doing.

BULLYING FORMS

QUESTION & REFLECTION

  • Have you ever given someone an insulting nickname?
  • Did you gang up with others to exclude or isolate someone in your class
  • Have you ever done pranks that you think are interesting, but annoy others?
  • Have you ever discussed the false gossip of a classmate with others?
  • Have you abused or ridiculed someone because of their different skin colour?
  • Have you ever spread false rumours about others?
  • Have you ever maliciously pulled the hair of a girl in your class?
  • Have you ever neglected a school bullying incident because you want to protect your personal safety?
  • Have you ever joined a bullying team because you didn't want to be bullied
  • Have you ever ignore someone who is bullied due to your personal feud with him

WHAT CAN WE DO AS THE BYSTANDER?

  • Defending the target of the bullying
  • Intervening as a group
  • Changing the subject
  • Questioning the bullying behaviour
  • Using humour to lighten up a serious situation
  • Openly stating an objection to bullying
  • Indicating approval of the victim and validating his or her social status
  • Reaching out privately to the target of the bullying to express support or concern.
  • Reporting the bullying to a trusted adult, parent, teacher, or school administrator.
  • Reaching out privately to the person doing the bullying to express concern, if they feel safe to do so.
Whatever form bullying takes, being bullied can have a huge impact on a young person’s life, impacting on their self-esteem and mental health. Sometimes someone may try to justify their behaviour by finding something different about the person they are bullying – this might include what they look like, how they express themselves or what they do. But this does not mean that the person being bullied is to blame. There is never any justification for bullying.
